# One-point projections of the quintic del Pezzo fivefold

## Outcome

Every finite one-point projection

\[
V_5=\operatorname{Gr}(2,5)\cap\mathbf P^8\longrightarrow
W\subset\mathbf P^7
\]

is incompatible with containment in a smooth degree-nine hypersurface in
\(\mathbf P^9\).  There are two orbits of external projection centers.  In
both, the image is a prime fivefold of degree five generated by five cubics,
and all five cubics vanish to order at least two along a linear
\(\mathbf P^3\subset W\).  This forces every degree-nine equation containing
\(W\) to have a singular point on that \(\mathbf P^3\).

This eliminates the eight-section residue in the smooth-normalization,
\(b=7\), five-dimensional rationally connected fiber analysis.  It is not an
application of the ordinary low-generator lemma: in \(\mathbf P^9\), the
ideal has two linear and five cubic generators, hence seven generators in
total.

The complete nine-section system is excluded separately by the exact Chern
number

\[
\int_{V_5}c_4\bigl(N^*_{V_5/\mathbf P^9}(9)\bigr)H=15856.
\]

The calculation is included below so that this integer does not depend on a
black-box localization run; it is also reproduced independently by
[`tools/v5_chern.py`](../tools/v5_chern.py).

## The full-system Chern number

Let \(G=\operatorname{Gr}(2,5)\), let \(S\) be its tautological rank-two
bundle, and put

\[
H=c_1(S^*)=\sigma_1,\qquad P=c_2(S^*)=\sigma_{1,1}.
\]

The splitting principle applied to \(T_G=S^*\otimes Q\) gives

\[
c_1(T_G)=5H,\qquad
c_2(T_G)=11H^2+P,\qquad
c_3(T_G)=15H^3.
\]

From

\[
0\longrightarrow T_G\longrightarrow T_{\mathbf P^9}|_G
\longrightarrow N_{G/\mathbf P^9}\longrightarrow0,
\qquad c(T_{\mathbf P^9}|_G)=(1+H)^{10},
\]

one obtains

\[
c_1(N)=5H,\qquad c_2(N)=9H^2-P,\qquad c_3(N)=5H^3.
\]

Write \(n_1,n_2,n_3\) for the formal Chern roots of \(N\).  Since
\(V_5\) is a hyperplane section of \(G\), the four formal roots of

\[
E=N^*_{V_5/\mathbf P^9}(9)
\]

are \(8H,9H-n_1,9H-n_2,9H-n_3\).  Therefore

\[
\begin{aligned}
c_4(E)
&=8H\prod_{j=1}^3(9H-n_j)\\
&=8H\bigl(400H^3-9HP\bigr).
\end{aligned}
\]

Finally, \([V_5]=H\) on \(G\), while Schubert--Pieri gives

\[
\int_GH^6=5,\qquad \int_GH^4P=2.
\]

For the second identity, the coefficient is the number of standard tableaux
of the skew shape \((3,3)/(1,1)\), namely the two tableaux of a \(2\times2\)
rectangle.  Hence

\[
\begin{aligned}
\int_{V_5}c_4(E)H
 &=8\int_GH^3(400H^3-9HP)\\
 &=8(400\cdot5-9\cdot2)=15856.
\end{aligned}
\]

The ideal of \(V_5\) in \(\mathbf P^9\) is generated by one linear form and
five Plücker quadrics, so

\[
\mathcal O(8)\oplus\mathcal O(7)^5\twoheadrightarrow E.
\]

Thus \(E\) is ample.  Fulton--Lazarsfeld positivity also gives the strict
positivity of the displayed Chern number without computing it.  A nowhere
zero normal differential of a smooth containing hypersurface would instead
give \(0\to\mathcal O\to E\) with locally free rank-three cokernel, forcing
\(c_4(E)=0\).  This excludes the complete nine-section system.

## Coordinates for \(V_5\)

Let

\[
V=U\oplus\langle e_5\rangle,\qquad
\alpha=e_1^*\wedge e_2^*+e_3^*\wedge e_4^*.
\]

The smooth hyperplane section is

\[
V_5=\operatorname{Gr}(2,V)\cap\mathbf P(\ker\alpha).
\]

Set

\[
a=p_{34}=-p_{12},\ b=p_{13},\ c=p_{14},\ d=p_{15},\
e=p_{23},\ f=p_{24},\ g=p_{25},\ h=p_{35},\ i=p_{45}.
\]

Its ideal in \(\mathbf P^8\) is

\[
\begin{aligned}
Q_1&=-a^2-bf+ce,&
Q_2&=-ah-bg+de,\\
Q_3&=-ai-cg+df,&
Q_4&=bi-ch+da,\\
Q_5&=ei-fh+ga.
\end{aligned}
\]

## Why two center representatives suffice

Every point of \(\mathbf P(\ker\alpha)\) can be written

\[
p=s+u\wedge e_5,\qquad s\in\Lambda^2_0U,\quad u\in U.
\]

The stabilizer of \([\alpha]\) contains \(\operatorname{GSp}(U,\alpha)\),
independent scaling of \(e_5\), and the shears
\(x\mapsto x+\varphi(x)e_5\).  A shear changes \(u\) by the image of
contraction with \(s\).

- If \(s^2\ne0\), contraction with \(s\) is invertible, so a shear kills
  \(u\).  The conformal symplectic group is transitive on the nonisotropic
  projective points of \(\Lambda^2_0U\).  A representative is
  \(-e_{12}+e_{34}\), or \([a=1]\).
- If \(s^2=0\) and \(s\ne0\), then \(s=x\wedge y\) for a Lagrangian plane
  \(L=\langle x,y\rangle\).  The bivector \(p\) is decomposable exactly when
  \(u\in L\).  Thus an external center has \(u\notin L\).  Shears change
  \(u\) by elements of \(L\), and the stabilizer of \(L\) is transitive on
  nonzero projective classes in \(U/L\).  A representative is
  \(e_{13}+e_{25}\), or \([b=g=1]\).
- If \(s=0\), then \(p=u\wedge e_5\) is decomposable and lies on \(V_5\), so
  it is not an external center.

Hence the two displayed representatives exhaust the external-center orbits.

## Exact eliminations

### Nonisotropic orbit

Project from \([a=1]\), equivalently eliminate \(a\).  The image ideal in
\(\mathbf Q[b,c,d,e,f,g,h,i]\) is generated by

\[
\begin{aligned}
G_1&=dfh-cgh-dei+bgi,\\
G_2&=dfg-cg^2-fhi+ei^2,\\
G_3&=deg-bg^2-fh^2+ehi,\\
G_4&=d^2f-cdg-chi+bi^2,\\
G_5&=d^2e-bdg-ch^2+bhi.
\end{aligned}
\]

Every \(G_j\) belongs to \((d,g,h,i)^2\).  Thus

\[
R_0=V(d,g,h,i)\simeq\mathbf P^3
\]

lies in the image and \(dG_j|_{R_0}=0\) for every \(j\).

### Isotropic orbit

For the representative \([b=g=1]\), put \(b=u\), \(g=u+v\), and eliminate
\(u\).  The image ideal in \(\mathbf Q[a,v,c,d,e,f,h,i]\) is generated by

\[
\begin{aligned}
H_1&=adf-cfh-a^2i+cei,\\
H_2&=acd-c^2h-vci+dfi-ai^2,\\
H_3&=a^2d-ach-avi+fhi-ei^2,\\
H_4&=a^2c-c^2e-vcf+df^2-afi,\\
H_5&=a^3-ace-avf+f^2h-efi.
\end{aligned}
\]

Every \(H_j\) belongs to \((a,c,f,i)^2\).  Hence

\[
R_1=V(a,c,f,i)\simeq\mathbf P^3
\]

has the same rank-zero property.

In both cases the script certifies that the elimination ideal is prime, has
projective dimension five and degree five, and has minimal graded resolution

\[
0\longrightarrow S(-5)\longrightarrow S(-4)^5
\longrightarrow S(-3)^5\longrightarrow S
\longrightarrow S/I_W\longrightarrow0.
\]

## The hypersurface obstruction

Regard \(W\subset\mathbf P^7\) as a subvariety of \(\mathbf P^9\), and let
\(L_1,L_2\) cut out its linear span.  In either orbit,

\[
I_{W/\mathbf P^9}=(L_1,L_2,G_1,\ldots,G_5),
\]

where the \(G_j\) denote the corresponding five cubics.  Every degree-nine
equation containing \(W\) has the form

\[
F=A_1L_1+A_2L_2+\sum_{j=1}^5B_jG_j,
\qquad \deg A_1=\deg A_2=8.
\]

On the relevant \(R\simeq\mathbf P^3\), both \(G_j\) and \(dG_j\) vanish,
so

\[
dF|_R=A_1\,dL_1+A_2\,dL_2.
\]

The two degree-eight forms \(A_1|_R,A_2|_R\) have a common projective zero.
At that point \(dF=0\), and the hypersurface \(V(F)\) is singular.  Therefore
no projected \(V_5\) of this kind can lie in the smooth degree-nine
hypersurface under study.

## Reproduction

From the repository root, run

```sh
M2 --script experiments/projected_v5_fivefold.m2
python3 tools/v5_chern.py
```

With Macaulay2 1.26.06 the script prints the two prime/dimension/degree
certificates, the common Betti table

```text
       0 1 2 3
total: 1 5 5 1
    0: 1 . . .
    1: . . . .
    2: . 5 5 1
```

and the two rank-zero linear spaces
\((d,g,h,i)\) and \((a,c,f,i)\).

The Python command prints

```text
integral_V5_c4_conormal9_times_H=15856
```
