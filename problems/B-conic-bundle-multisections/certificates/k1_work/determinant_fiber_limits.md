# Limits of the determinant-fiber shortcut

## Verdict

The theta-characteristic description does **not** prove the uniform
determinant-fiber bounds that the earlier equigeneric strategy would have
needed in the square-free three-`t=2` row.  In rank three that bound was

\[
 f_3(C)\leq 5,
\]

under a hypothetical genus-at-most-four theorem.  In rank two the required
total bound would have been `f_2(C)<=8`; after quotienting by the
three-dimensional congruence orbit (whose dimension is verified in Section
4), this is again a bound of five.

There is a useful rigorous reduction.  A fixed self-dual sheaf with the
displayed Euler-bundle resolution contributes only finitely many projective
quadratic forms.  Positive-dimensional fibers must therefore come from
families of **varying local types** of self-dual rank-one torsion-free
sheaves.  Piontkowski proves finiteness after the local type is fixed, but his
results give no upper bound on the dimension of the space of local types.
In fact, his simplest non-ADE family remains two-generated, so the local
two-generator condition supplied by a `2 x 2` matrix does not restore
finiteness.

There is also a precise limitation on unrestricted first-order deformation
arguments.  At every nonzero-determinant point of a natural three-center jet
stratum the projective fixed-determinant tangent space has dimension at least
six.  This does **not** exhibit a six-dimensional determinant fiber: the extra
tangent vectors can be quadratically obstructed.  An exact witness shows that
the stratum does contain a reduced determinant, so the issue is not an artifact
of nonreduced curves.  It shows that an `Ext^1` or differential-rank argument
alone cannot prove the desired bound five.

Thus a proof by that strategy would need a nonlinear theorem about the locally
closed Euler-resolution locus, stratified by the local symmetric matrix
factorizations (and, in rank two, by the exceptional divisor).  No such
theorem was found in the cited primary sources.

## 1. Rank three: reduction to resolution-compatible local types

Put

\[
 E=\Omega^1_{\mathbf P^2}(1),\qquad
 W=H^0\!\left(\operatorname{Sym}^2E^\vee\otimes O(3)\right),
 \qquad \dim W=42.
\]

For `q in W` with reduced nonzero determinant
`C=(det(q)=0)`, set `M=L(-3)` as in
[`three_center_equigeneric_attempt.md`](three_center_equigeneric_attempt.md).
Then

\[
 0\longrightarrow E(-3)\xrightarrow{q}E^\vee
 \longrightarrow i_*M\longrightarrow0,                       \tag{1.1}
\]

\[
 M\simeq\mathcal Hom_C(M,\omega_C),\qquad h^0(C,M)=3.          \tag{1.2}
\]

Locally, (1.1) is the cokernel of a symmetric `2 x 2` matrix, so
`M` is generated by at most two elements at every point.

### A fixed sheaf gives only finitely many projective forms

The bundle `E` is stable and simple, hence

\[
 \operatorname{Aut}(E)=\operatorname{Aut}(E^\vee)=\mathbf G_m.
\]

Bott's formula (or the Euler sequence) gives

\[
 \operatorname{Ext}^1(E^\vee,E(-3))
 =H^1(E\otimes E(-3))=0.                                      \tag{1.3}
\]

Suppose two sequences of the form (1.1) have isomorphic cokernel sheaves.
Compose the first quotient map with an isomorphism of the cokernels.  By
(1.3), that map lifts across the second quotient to an endomorphism of
`E^vee`.  Repeating the argument in the opposite direction shows that the
two composites differ from the identity by maps from `E^vee` to `E(-3)`.
But

\[
 \operatorname{Hom}(E^\vee,E(-3))=H^0(E\otimes E(-3))=0,
\]

so the lifts are inverse automorphisms.  Simplicity makes each a scalar, and
the induced map on the kernels is scalar as well.  The two maps `q` are
therefore projectively equal.  Consequently, a fixed isomorphism class of
`M` contributes at most one point (in particular, only finitely many points)
to the projective determinant fiber.

It follows that the dimension of a determinant fiber is bounded by the
dimension of the locus of isomorphism classes of sheaves `M` satisfying
(1.1)-(1.2).  This is much smaller than the full compactified Jacobian, but
it is not controlled by the usual smooth theta-characteristic
correspondence.

### Fixed local type is finite

For a reduced Gorenstein curve, Piontkowski defines the local type of a
rank-one torsion-free sheaf as the collection of its modules at the singular
points.  Proposition 1.6 of

> J. Piontkowski, *Theta-characteristics on singular curves*, J. London
> Math. Soc. (2) **75** (2007), 479--494,
> [doi:10.1112/jlms/jdm021](https://doi.org/10.1112/jlms/jdm021),
> [author PDF](https://www.math.uni-duesseldorf.de/~piontkow/Theta.pdf),

says that the group of two-torsion line bundles acts transitively on the
theta-characteristics of a fixed local type.  In characteristic zero this
two-torsion group is finite: multiplication by two has finite kernel on the
abelian and toric parts of the generalized Jacobian and is an automorphism
on its additive part.  Hence there are only finitely many
theta-characteristics of each fixed local type.

This identifies the exact source of possible positive-dimensional fibers:
the local type itself must vary.  It also explains why the vanishing (1.3)
does not prove a fiber bound.  That vanishing controls the presentation once
`M` is fixed; it says nothing about the dimension of the family of possible
`M`'s.

## 2. A two-generated family of varying self-dual local types

Piontkowski's local theorem is deliberately a finiteness theorem, not a
dimension theorem:

> J. Piontkowski, *Self-dual modules over local rings of curve
> singularities*, J. Pure Appl. Algebra **207** (2006), 327--339,
> [doi:10.1016/j.jpaa.2005.10.001](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jpaa.2005.10.001),
> [author PDF](https://www.math.uni-duesseldorf.de/~piontkow/Selfdual.pdf).

Its main theorem says that a reduced curve singularity has finite
self-dual Cohen--Macaulay representation type precisely when its local ring
dominates a plane ADE singularity.  Outside that class, the proof constructs
one-parameter families of pairwise nonisomorphic self-dual modules.  It does
not give an upper bound for the dimension of all such families.

The extra two-generator condition in (1.1) does not remove the phenomenon.
Specialize Case 1a of the proof to the reduced Gorenstein plane branch

\[
 R=\mathbf C[[t^4,t^5]]
   \simeq \mathbf C[[x,y]]/(y^4-x^5),
 \qquad \widetilde R=\mathbf C[[t]].
\]

For all but finitely many `lambda`, Piontkowski's modules

\[
 M_\lambda
 =\langle 1,t^2+\lambda t^3\rangle+t^4\widetilde R             \tag{2.1}
\]

are pairwise nonisomorphic and self-dual.  They are already generated by
the two displayed elements as `R`-modules.  Indeed, put
`g=t^2+lambda*t^3`, `x=t^4`, and `y=t^5`.  Then

\[
 t^7=yg-\lambda x^2,
 \qquad
 t^6=xg-\lambda t^7.
\]

The generator `1` gives `t^4,t^5`, the generator `g` therefore gives
`t^6,t^7`, and multiplication by `t^4` generates every remaining monomial
of `t^4\widetilde R`.  Thus

\[
 M_\lambda=R+Rg.
\]

Proposition 1.3(3) of the 2007 paper also shows that, on an irreducible
curve, any prescribed collection of self-dual local rank-one modules occurs
as the local type of a global theta-characteristic.  Therefore neither
local self-duality nor two-generation can support a finiteness argument.

This example must not be overstated.  It does **not** show that the modules
(2.1) satisfy `h^0=3`, admit the exact global resolution (1.1), or occur over
a determinant octic in the final three-center incidence.  The exact Euler
Betti table is an additional locally closed condition and can cut a local
family to a finite set.  Conversely, no result in either Piontkowski paper
bounds the subfamily on which that Betti table occurs.  The desired
inequality `f_3<=5` is therefore neither proved nor disproved by (2.1).

## 3. Why a tangent-space or `Ext^1` bound cannot by itself give five

Work in a local trivialization and write

\[
 q=\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\ b&c\end{pmatrix},\qquad
 r=\begin{pmatrix}\alpha&\beta\\ \beta&\gamma\end{pmatrix}.
\]

The differential of the determinant is

\[
 d(\det)_q(r)=c\alpha-2b\beta+a\gamma.                         \tag{3.1}
\]

Thus its image lies in the ideal

\[
 I_q=(a,b,c),                                                   \tag{3.2}
\]

the rank-zero scheme of the quadratic form.  At a singular point where
`q` has rank one, `I_q=O` and this source of tangent growth is absent.  At a
rank-zero point it can be substantial.  This distinction is invisible if
one records only the singularity or normalization genus of `det(q)`.

Here is a concrete obstruction to a purely first-order argument on the
ambient jet stratum.  Fix three distinct proper points `p_1,p_2,p_3`, and
consider the jet condition that every entry of `q` have zero constant and
linear term at each point:

\[
 a,b,c\in\mathfrak m_{p_i}^2\quad(i=1,2,3).                    \tag{3.3}
\]

Then `det(q)` has multiplicity at least four at each `p_i`, as required of
three proper `t=2` centers.  This jet stratum is nonempty even on the reduced
determinant locus.  The exact script
[`tangent_stratum_reduced_example.py`](tangent_stratum_reduced_example.py)
uses the Euler-kernel frames at the three coordinate points.  It finds that
the 27 doubled-jet conditions have rank 27 in the 60-dimensional space of
symmetric cubic matrices (nullity 33, including the 18-dimensional kernel of
restriction to `W`), constructs an explicit member, and verifies

\[
 \gcd(B,B_x,B_y,B_z)=1.
\]

Thus its octic determinant is reduced.  The exact output is
[`tangent_stratum_reduced_example.log`](tangent_stratum_reduced_example.log).
The initial form has degree exactly four at each coordinate point.  This
witness is used only for the tangent-space limitation: it is not asserted to
have no other essential centers or to satisfy the two rationality-row cluster
vanishings.
Let

\[
 Z=\coprod_{i=1}^3\operatorname{Spec}
   (O_{\mathbf P^2,p_i}/\mathfrak m_{p_i}^2).
\]

Each doubled point has length three, so `length(Z)=9`.  First jets at three
distinct points impose independent conditions on degree-eight forms; hence

\[
 h^0(I_Z(8))=45-9=36.                                          \tag{3.4}
\]

Equations (3.1)-(3.3) imply

\[
 \operatorname{im}\bigl(d(\det)_q:W\to H^0(O(8))\bigr)
 \subseteq H^0(I_Z(8)).
\]

Therefore

\[
 \operatorname{rank}d(\det)_q\le36,
 \qquad
 \dim\ker d(\det)_q\ge42-36=6.                                \tag{3.5}
\]

If `f=det(q)` is nonzero, the tangent space to the **projective** fiber over
`[f]` is

\[
 \{r:d(\det)_q(r)\in\mathbf C f\}/\mathbf C q.
\]

Euler's identity gives `d(det)_q(q)=2f`, so this space has dimension exactly
`dim ker d(det)_q`, and (3.5) gives the lower bound six.

This remains a tangent-space statement only, including at the explicit
reduced member just exhibited.  The determinant map is quadratic:
for a first-order kernel vector `r`, the second-order term is `det(r)`.
Those quadratic terms can obstruct all or most of the six tangent
directions, and a scheme can have a six-dimensional Zariski tangent space at
an isolated nonreduced point.  Consequently (3.5) is **not** a
counterexample to `f_3(C)<=5` and is not evidence for an actual
six-dimensional component.  It proves the narrower methodological point:
an `Ext^1` computation or differential-rank estimate, without the nonlinear
obstruction equations, cannot establish the desired uniform fiber bound even
on the reduced Euler-resolution locus.

The all-three-proper-center subcase is eliminated by
[`proper_three_center_theorem.md`](proper_three_center_theorem.md).  That does
not change the lesson for a proposed general
theta-fiber theorem: infinitely-near strata have analogous rank-zero ideals,
and their nonlinear matrix-factorization equations must be retained rather
than replaced by the normalization genus.

## 4. Rank two is not covered by ordinary theta theory

For the canonical rank-two triple, on

\[
 Z=\operatorname{Bl}_b\mathbf P^2,
 \qquad K=O_Z\oplus O_Z(-(H-R)),
\]

the coefficient space again has dimension 42.  The group

\[
 \operatorname{Aut}(K)/\mathbf G_m
\]

has dimension three and acts by congruence while preserving the projective
determinant.  Thus a total bound `f_2<=8` is equivalent to bounding the
quotient family of compatible self-dual sheaves by five, exactly the same
numerical problem as in rank three.

Here the orbit really has dimension three on the reduced nonzero-determinant
locus relevant to the frontier.  Write an automorphism, modulo its central
scalar, as

\[
 g=\begin{pmatrix}1&\ell\\0&r\end{pmatrix},
 \qquad \ell\in H^0(O_Z(H-R)).
\]

For a form `q=[[c,b],[b,a]]`, reduced nonzero determinant implies `c!=0`,
since `c=0` would give the square `det(q)=-b^2`.  Projective stabilization
then first gives `c*ell=(1-r)b`.  A positive-dimensional noncentral stabilizer
forces `b=c*ell_0`; the remaining equation forces `a=c*ell_0^2`, and hence
`det(q)=0`.  Thus every reduced nonzero-determinant form has finite stabilizer
modulo the center and a three-dimensional congruence orbit.

There are two additional difficulties.

First, on a reduced strict transform of multiplicity exactly two at `b`, the
duality furnished by the symmetric resolution is twisted by

\[
 O_C(C-3H)=O_C(5H-2R),                                        \tag{4.1}
\]

whereas

\[
 \omega_C=O_C(K_Z+C)=O_C(5H-R).                               \tag{4.2}
\]

It is therefore not literally the ordinary theta-characteristic involution
treated in the cited papers.  Locally the invertible twist does not eliminate
the varying-local-type issue, but globally it must be retained in any count.

Second, if the plane octic has multiplicity `m>2` at `b`, then its divisor as
a member of `|8H-2R|` is

\[
 C_{\mathrm{strict}}+(m-2)R.
\]

For `m=3`, this can still be a reduced Cartier (hence Gorenstein) divisor, so
Piontkowski's reduced local theory is not automatically inapplicable; the
global exceptional twist (4.1)-(4.2) and component gluing must nevertheless be
retained.  For `m>=4`, the exceptional curve occurs with multiplicity at least
two, so the determinant divisor is nonreduced and lies outside the reduced
Gorenstein setup of the 2007 paper.  A theorem only about torsion-free sheaves
on the reduced strict transform would therefore leave part of the rank-two
frontier untreated.

## 5. The exact theorem this route would have needed

For a genus-at-most-four replacement of the failed curve lemma, the
equigeneric strategy would still require both of the following new results.

1. For every relevant reduced plane octic `C`, the locally closed locus of
   rank-one self-dual sheaves admitting the exact Euler resolution (1.1) has
   dimension at most five.
2. On `Bl_b(P^2)`, the analogous exceptional-twisted locus, modulo
   `Aut(K)/G_m`, has dimension at most five, including the strata with an
   exceptional and possibly nonreduced determinant component.

Beauville's smooth determinantal correspondence and Piontkowski's
fixed-local-type counts do not imply either statement.  Piontkowski's local
families explain why the local types must be stratified; (3.5) explains why
the nonlinear obstruction equations must also be used.  The later marked
singleton and separator incidences supplied the direct route and completed
the class-`(1,1)` exclusion.  These standalone fiber statements remain
unproved, but they are no longer missing inputs to that theorem.

For comparison, Beauville's smooth correspondence is in A. Beauville,
*Determinantal hypersurfaces*, Michigan Math. J. **48** (2000), 39--64,
[author PDF](https://math.univ-cotedazur.fr/u/beauvill/pubs/det.pdf),
especially Section 4.  Remark 4.4 observes that arbitrary plane curves admit
some symmetric linear determinantal representation, but it neither fixes the
Euler resolution above nor bounds representation fibers over singular
curves.
